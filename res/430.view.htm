675<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140161358698688title">

676Abstract</h2>

677<!--article-meta-->

678<div>

679<div id="S1" class="sec sec-first">

680<h3 id="S1title">

681Background</h3>

682<p id="P1" class="p p-first-last">

683Current arthritis treatments often have side-effects attributable to active compounds as well as route of administration. Cannabidiol (CBD) attenuates inflammation and pain without side-effects, but CBD is hydrophobic and has poor oral bioavailability. Topical drug application avoids gastrointestinal administration, first pass metabolism, providing more constant plasma levels.</p>

684</div>

685<div id="S2" class="sec">

686<h3 id="S2title">

687Methods</h3>

688<p id="P2" class="p p-first-last">

689This study examined efficacy of transdermal CBD for reduction in inflammation and pain, assessing any adverse effects in a rat complete Freund&#x02019;s adjuvant-induced monoarthritic knee joint model. CBD gels (0.6, 3.1, 6.2 or 62.3 mg/day) were applied for 4 consecutive days after arthritis induction. Joint circumference and immune cell invasion in histological sections were measured to indicate level of inflammation. Paw withdrawal latency (PWL) in response to noxious heat stimulation determined nociceptive sensitization, and exploratory behaviour ascertained animal&#x02019;s activity level.</p>

690</div>

691<div id="S3" class="sec">

692<h3 id="S3title">

693Results</h3>

694<p id="P3" class="p p-first-last">

695Measurement of plasma CBD concentration provided by transdermal absorption revealed linearity with 0.6&#x02013;6.2 mg/day doses. Transdermal CBD gel significantly reduced joint swelling, limb posture scores as a rating of spontaneous pain, immune cell infiltration and thickening of the synovial membrane in a dose-dependent manner. PWL recovered to near baseline level. Immunohistochemical analysis of spinal cord (CGRP, OX42) and dorsal root ganglia (TNF&#x003b1;) revealed dose-dependent reductions of pro-inflammatory biomarkers. Results showed 6.2 and 62 mg/day were effective doses. Exploratory behaviour was not altered by CBD indicating limited effect on higher brain function.</p>

696</div>

697<div id="S4" class="sec sec-last">

698<h3 id="S4title">

699Conclusions</h3>

700<p id="P4" class="p p-first-last">

701These data indicate that topical CBD application has therapeutic potential for relief of arthritis pain-related behaviours and inflammation without evident side-effects.</p>

702</div>

703</div>

705<div id="S5" class="tsec sec">

706<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S5title">

7071. Introduction</h2>

708<p id="P5" class="p p-first">

709Almost 50 million (22.2%) adult Americans over 18 were diagnosed with arthritis in 2007&#x02013;2009, most prominently osteoarthritis and the autoimmune disease rheumatoid arthritis. A projected increase to 67 million is anticipated by 2030 (<a href="#R11" rid="R11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394197">

710Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), 2010</a>

711). The most effective treatment for rheumatoid arthritis is injectable fusion-proteins which sequester the most prominent proinflammatory cytokine tumour necrosis factor &#x003b1; (TNF&#x003b1;). These chimeric antibodies may halt progression of the disease, but side-effects include immune suppression (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394195">

712Crawford and Curtis, 2008</a>

713; <a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394225">

714Furst, 2010</a>

715; <a href="#R26" rid="R26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394184">

716Hastings et al., 2010</a>

717). Neurogenic drive also contributes to severity of arthritic inflammation (<a href="#R56" rid="R56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394220">

718Sluka et al., 1994</a>

719), and may contribute to its reoccurrence.</p>

720<p id="P6">

721Cannabinoids and cannabinoid receptors are potential targets for reducing pain and inflammation (<a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394194">

722Clayton et al., 2002</a>

723; <a href="#R49" rid="R49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394226">

724Richardson et al., 2008</a>

725; <a href="#R68" rid="R68" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394176">

726Zuardi, 2008</a>

727). Cannabis sativa contains approximately 80 different cannabinoids of which &#x00394;9-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and cannabidiol (CBD) are primary (<a href="#R42" rid="R42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394177">

728Mechoulam and Shvo, 1963</a>

729; <a href="#R40" rid="R40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394175">

730Mechoulam, 1970</a>

731; <a href="#R61" rid="R61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394211">

732Turner et al., 1980</a>

733). These compounds are chemically similar to endogenous endocannabinoid lipid derivatives including anandamide (arachidonoylethanolamide) and 2-arachidonoylglycerol. At present THC and CBD are available combined in the oral spray Sativex<sup>

734&#x000ae;</sup>

735 (GW Pharmaceuticals plc, Cambridge, UK) which is prescribed to adult patients for neuropathic pain. Psychoactive THC side-effects are experienced, however, and long-term use of cannabis sativa has been shown to increase risk of developing psychosis and schizophrenia (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394182">

736Berman et al., 2004</a>

737; <a href="#R38" rid="R38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394217">

738Malone et al., 2010</a>

739). Research is ongoing to find better, effective cannabinoids and better routes of application.</p>

740<p id="P7" class="p p-last">

741CBD, while structurally similar to THC, is a non-psychoactive cannabinoid with therapeutic potential for treatment of neuropathic pain, cancer pain, multiple sclerosis and inflammation (<a href="#R41" rid="R41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394230">

742Mechoulam and Hanus, 2002</a>

743; <a href="#R43" rid="R43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394247">

744Mechoulam et al., 2002</a>

745; <a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394251">

746Burstein and Zurier, 2009</a>

747). Oral bioavailability of CBD is very limited, due to first pass metabolism during digestion (<a href="#R41" rid="R41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394215">

748Mechoulam and Hanus, 2002</a>

749). The THC-like cannabinoids act at CB1 or CB2 receptors, whereas CBD-like cannabinoids have little binding affinity, leaving their role in inhibition incompletely understood. Data suggest <em>

750in vitro</em>

751 application of CBD inhibits signalling through GPR55 and TRP channel superfamily members and <em>

752in vivo</em>

753 oral administration is dose-dependently reducing pro-inflammatory cytokine release (<a href="#R13" rid="R13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394200">

754Coffey et al., 1996</a>

755; <a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394210">

756Malfait et al., 2000</a>

757; <a href="#R2" rid="R2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_827220749">

758Akopian et al., 2008</a>

759; <a href="#R63" rid="R63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394241">

760Whyte et al., 2009</a>

761). More recently, CBD was successfully delivered transdermally in different species for anti-inflammatory activity (<a href="#R35" rid="R35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394256">

762Lodzki et al., 2003</a>

763; <a href="#R59" rid="R59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394243">

764Stinchcomb et al., 2004</a>

765; <a href="#R47" rid="R47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394262">

766Paudel et al., 2010</a>

767). In this study, <em>

768in vivo</em>

769 efficacy of transdermal CBD delivery to reduce inflammation and pain-related behaviours is tested in a rat adjuvant-induced monoarthritis model with both inflammatory and neurogenic properties (<a href="#R56" rid="R56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394216">

770Sluka et al., 1994</a>

771). Nociceptive behaviour, potential adverse side-effects and inflammation-associated anatomical changes in knee joint and neuronal tissue were assessed.</p>

772</div>

773<div id="S6" class="tsec sec">

774<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S6title">

7752. Materials and methods</h2>

776<div id="S7" class="sec sec-first">

777<h3 id="S7title">

7782.1 Animals</h3>

779<p id="P8" class="p p-first-last">

780All animal procedures were approved by the University of Kentucky IACUC committee and were conducted according to guidelines for the ethical treatment of experimental animals published by the Internal Association for the Study of Pain. All experiments were conducted using 260&#x02013;280 g male Sprague&#x02013;Dawley rats (Harlan Laboratories, Indianapolis, IN, USA) housed in individual cages on a 12 h/12 h dark/light reversed cycle and allowed access to food and water <em>

781ad libitum</em>

782. A total of 54 rats were used in the experiments described here of which 21 were used as naive controls and 23 were subjected to adjuvant-induced arthritis. To induce monoarthritis, animals were anesthetized (2&#x02013;4% isoflurane) and one knee joint injected with 100 &#x003bc;L complete Freud&#x02019;s adjuvant (CFA) (DIFCO Laboratories, Detroit, MI, USA) emulsion (2 mg/mL diluted in 1:1 normal saline:peanut oil). Rats were returned to their home cages and monitored daily. Joint circumference and pain-related behaviours were assessed prior to CFA injection and daily beginning on day 3 after CFA (days 3&#x02013;7).</p>

783</div>

784<div id="S8" class="sec">

785<h3 id="S8title">

7862.2 Gel preparation</h3>

787<p id="P9" class="p p-first-last">

788All gels, including vehicle controls, were prepared by weighing the desired amount of CBD (gift from NIDA) and dissolving it in ethanol (72.5% w/w). Once dissolved, nanopure water (Barntead NANOpure<sup>

789&#x000ae;</sup>

790 Diamond<sup>

791&#x02122;</sup>

792 ultrapure filtration system, Dubuque, IA, USA) was added followed by isopropyl myristate (Fisher Scientific, Fairlawn, NJ, USA). Carbopol<sup>

793&#x000ae;</sup>

794 980 polymere (Noveon Inc., Cleveland, OH, USA) was added (0.9% w/w) and the solution sonicated for 10 min to ensure complete incorporation of the Carbopol<sup>

795&#x000ae;</sup>

796 980. Polymerization of Carbopol <sup>

797&#x000ae;</sup>

798 980 to form the hydroalcoholic gel was initiated by adding sodium hydroxide (0.1 N). Gels were then sonicated for 10 min, loaded into 1 mL syringes and sealed. Gels made just prior to the initial dosing were used for the entire week since no degradation was observed and plasma CBD concentration remained constant.</p>

799</div>

800<div id="S9" class="sec">

801<h3 id="S9title">

8022.3 Assessment of joint inflammation</h3>

803<p id="P10" class="p p-first-last">

804Hindlimbs were fully extended while the circumference of the knee joint was determined using a flexible tape measure wrapped around the centre of the joint. Skin temperature over the joint was measured at the patella with an infrared temperature probe (Fluke, Wilmington, North Carolina, USA). Measurements were taken on day 0, 3 and 7.</p>

805</div>

806<div id="S10" class="sec">

807<h3 id="S10title">

8082.4 Behavioural assays</h3>

809<div id="S11" class="sec sec-first">

810<p>

811</p>

812<h4 id="S11title" class="inline">

8132.4.1 Spontaneous pain rating </h4>

814<p id="P11" class="p p-first-last">

815As a measure of spontaneous pain, limb posture was scored daily in the morning while animals were in their home cages by a scientist blinded to the animal&#x02019;s treatment. A subjective pain-related behavioural scale was used (<a href="#R55" rid="R55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394206">

816Sluka et al., 1993</a>

817) with 0 &#x02013; normal; 1 &#x02013; curling of the toes, 2 &#x02013; eversion of the paw; 3 &#x02013; partial weight bearing; 4 &#x02013; non-weight bearing and guarding and 5 &#x02013; avoidance of any contact with the hindlimb.</p>

818</div>

819<div id="S12" class="sec">

820<p>

821</p>

822<h4 id="S12title" class="inline">

8232.4.3 Hindpaw thermal hypersensitivity </h4>

824<p id="P12" class="p p-first-last">

825Hindpaw heat sensitivity was assessed prior to CFA injection as well as daily starting on day 3 after inflammation, 4 h after each gel application. Paw withdrawal latency (PWL) was measured as described in our previous study (<a href="#R65" rid="R65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394238">

826Zhang et al., 2002</a>

827). Briefly, rats were acclimated for 20&#x02013;30 min to individual plastic chambers (10 &#x000d7; 10 &#x000d7; 25 cm) on a glass-top table (2 mm thick). The light beam from a high-intensity projector lamp bulb (Quartzline Lamp, GE, Cleveland, OH, USA) was projected through a 5 &#x000d7; 10 mm aperture attached to an on/off switch with a digital timer. Maximal cut-off time for paw withdrawal reflex was set at 15 s to avoid skin damage. Both hindpaws were tested independently for 5 trials at 5 min intervals by an examiner blinded to the treatment group. The mean responses &#x000b1; standard error of the mean (SEM) are reported.</p>

828</div>

829<div id="S13" class="sec sec-last">

830<p>

831</p>

832<h4 id="S13title" class="inline">

8332.4.4 Exploratory activity </h4>

834<p id="P13" class="p p-first-last">

835Open-field exploratory activities were assessed in a Flexfield Animal Activity System (San Diego Instruments, San Diego, CA, USA) with Photobeam Activity System software coupled to a 486 computer (Hewlett Packard, Palo Alto, CA, USA). Animal behaviours include time spent in total exploratory activity (active time, distance travelled, total beams broken, rearing events, rearing time) and resting were recorded in a 40 &#x000d7; 40 &#x000d7; 40 cm transparent plexiglass box for 45 min prior to and at the end of the experiment (<a href="#R66" rid="R66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394249">

836Zhang et al., 2004</a>

837).</p>

838</div>

839</div>

840<div id="S14" class="sec">

841<h3 id="S14title">

8422.5 Gel administration</h3>

843<p id="P14" class="p p-first-last">

844On day 3 after induction of monoarthritis, the back of each animal was shaved and vehicle gel or gel containing 1 or 10% CBD was applied by rubbing it into the skin for 30 s. The area of gel application and volume used corresponded to the calculated final dose of CBD, i.e. 0.62 mg CBD was dosed by applying 75 &#x003bc;L of 1% CBD gel on 3.5 cm<sup>

8452</sup>

846 of shaved skin of the animals&#x02019; backs, 3.1 mg CBD by giving 375 &#x003bc;L of 1% CBD gel on 17.5 cm<sup>

8472</sup>

848, 6.2 mg equalled 750 &#x003bc;L of 1% CBD gel on 35.0 cm<sup>

8492</sup>

850, and 62 mg by rubbing 750 &#x003bc;L of 10% CBD gel onto the skin (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4851925/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid="">

851<span>

852Table 1</span>

853</a>

854). Joint inflammation and nociceptive behaviour were assessed starting 4 h after gel application. On the final day of gel application after completion of all behavioural assays, rats were killed by pentobarbital overdose, blood samples were collected for CBD plasma quantification and transcardially perfused with 4% paraformaldehyde.</p>

9392.6 Plasma extraction</h3>

940<p id="P15" class="p p-first-last">

941Plasma concentration of transdermally absorbed CBD was determined. As described by <a href="#R47" rid="R47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394239">

944g</em>

It was then reconstituted in 100 &#x003bc;L of ACN, vortexed and sonicated for 5 min. 948<h3 id="S16title">

9492.7 Analytical LC/MS method</h3>

950<p id="P16" class="p p-first-last">

951The LC/MS system used to analyse samples was comprised of a Waters Alliance 2695 pump, an autosampler, a Micromass ZQ detector and a 996 photodiode array detector with MassLynx software (Milford, MA, USA). A Symmetry<sup>

952&#x000ae;</sup>

953 C18 column (150 &#x000d7; 2.1 mm, 5 &#x003bc;m) with a Sentry Symmetry<sup>

954&#x000ae;</sup>

955 guard column (10 &#x000d7; 2.1 mm, 3.5 &#x003bc;m) was utilized with the LC/MS system. The ZQ detector was used with an electrospray ionization probe set for single ion monitoring for CBD quantification. Analysis was performed in negative mode for <em>

956m/z</em>

957 313 [CBD-H]-(dwell time: 0.3 s). Capillary and cone voltage were set at 35 kV and 40 V, respectively. Source block temperature was set at 120 &#x000b0;C, and desolvation temperature to 250 &#x000b0;C. Nitrogen was used as nebulization (flow rate: 50 l/h) and drying gas (flow rate: 450 l/h). The mobile phase was comprised of 75:25 ACN:2 mM ammonium acetate buffer w/5% ACN and used at a flow rate of 0.25 mL/min resulting in a mean CBD retention time of 5.6&#x02013;5.7 min. Standard curves were linear within the range 2&#x02013;300 ng/mL and concentrations of samples were determined by comparison.</p>

960<h3 id="S17title">

9612.8 Immunohistochemistry</h3>

962<p id="P17" class="p p-first">

963Spinal cord, dorsal root ganglia (DRG) and knee joint synovial joint capsule membranes were excised after transcardial perfusion, post-fixed with 4% buffered paraformaldehyde, cryoprotected overnight with 30% sucrose, embedded in OCT compound (Electron Microscopy Sciences, Hatfield, PA, USA) and stored at &#x02212;80 &#x000b0;C. Tissue was sectioned (14&#x02013;20 &#x003bc;m) with a cryostat at &#x02212;20 &#x000b0;C, sections collected on microscopic slides (Superfrost Plus, VWR, Radnor, PA) and stained. Synovial joint capsule membranes were stained with haematoxylin and eosin (HandE; Ricca Chemical Company, Arlington, TX, USA) and cover slipped using Permount (Fisher Scientific).</p>

964<p id="P18">

965Spinal cord sections were stained using monoclonal mouse anti-OX-42 (1:1,000, CD11b/c; Abcam, Cambridge, MA, USA) and rabbit anti-CGRP (1:2000; Bachem, Torrance, CA, USA) antibodies. Primary antibodies were detected using the appropriate Alexa Fluor conjugated secondary antibodies (Jackson ImmunoResearch, West Grove, PA, USA). Slides were coverslipped using Vectashield Hard Set mounting medium with DAPI (VectorLabs, Burlingame, CA, USA). Controls included the absence of staining upon omission of the primary antibody and the side to side differences between the ipsilateral and contralateral sides as internal controls.</p>

966<p id="P19">

967Images of haematoxylin and eosin (H&#x00026;E) staining were acquired using a Nikon Eclipse E1000 microscope equipped with a Nikon DXM 1200F digital camera and ACT-1 software. Synovial membrane thickness was measured by drawing a perpendicular line from the outer surface to the inner margin of the intimal layer of the synovial membrane in two places within each section and averaging their length (<a href="#R19" rid="R19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394193">

968Cunnane et al., 1999</a>

969).</p>

970<p id="P20" class="p p-last">

971Immunostaining of fluorescent labelled tissue was visualized using a Nikon Eclipse E1000 microscope equipped with a Cool Snap photometric Camera ES and analysed off-line with MetaMorph software. Camera settings were kept constant within an antibody staining to quantify and compare immunoreactivity. Images of the dorsal horn were analysed by outlining the substantia gelatinosa, determining and averaging the mean intensity of staining in this region. Background fluorescent intensity was measured outside of this area and subtracted. DRG sections were sampled by drawing regions of interest over five randomly selected sensory neuron somas, measuring the mean intensity and averaging for each animal sampled. Histological analysis was conducted in the four experimental groups: naive (uninjected control animals), CFA + VEH (monoarthritis treated with vehicle gel), CFA + low-dose CBD (monoarthritis treated with 0.62 or 3.1 mg/day CBD) and CFA + high-dose CBD (monoarthritis treated with 6.2 or 62.3 mg/day CBD). 1327<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S26title">

13284. Discussion</h2>

1329<div id="S27" class="sec sec-first">

1330<h3 id="S27title">

13314.1 Transdermal CBD bioactivity</h3>

1332<p id="P31" class="p p-first-last">

1333Outcomes of this study indicate that topical application of CBD gel is an effective treatment for reduction in inflammation and hypersensitivity associated with the rodent adjuvant-induced monoarthritis model. Transdermal administration of CBD provided good blood absorption due to avoidance of first pass metabolism encountered by orally administered drugs. <a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394231">

1334Malfait et al. (2000)</a>

1335 saw a bell-shaped dose-dependency curve for CBD given orally or intraperitoneally to attenuate nociceptive behaviour in a collagen/CFA-induced arthritis model in mice. Similarly in this study, CBD plasma concentrations for rats dosed with 0.6, 3.1 and 6.2 mg/day exhibited an excellent linear correlation. However, the highest dose, 62.3 mg per day, did not fit into the linear pharmacokinetic profile. Dosing was increased by massaging the total amount of CBD gel into a larger skin area on the back while gel concentration (1%) remained identical. A 10% gel formulation was used for the highest transdermal CBD treatment since skin area could not be appropriately increased. The 10% gel was close to solubility saturation and may have an increased absorption rate compared to 1% formulations. The lack of increased outcome for this highest CBD concentration was potentially due to maximally activated CBD effects or capacity-limit absorption and metabolism. This would account for the flattened linear pharmacokinetic profile effect of the 62.3 mg/day dose. Spreading large quantities of gel directly on the skin over the joint itself was not feasible in this transdermal dosing paradigm and would provide opportunity for oral ingestion by the rats. Application of 10% CBD gel on skin directly overlaying an inflamed joint in patients would potentially increase local CBD concentrations and enhance effectiveness with less systemic involvement.</p>

1336</div>

1337<div id="S28" class="sec">

1338<h3 id="S28title">

13394.2 Efficacy of transdermal CBD</h3>

1340<p id="P32" class="p p-first">

1341Efficacy of transdermal CBD for reduction in inflammation-associated symptoms in adjuvant-induced monoarthritic animals was determined comparing knee joint circumference and other features. The 6.2 mg/day dose optimally reduced swelling and increasing the CBD dose (62.3 mg/day) did not yield additional improvement. Likewise, increased synovial membrane thickness was reduced by the 6.2 mg/day CBD treatment. These results concur with previous studies showing orally administered CBD decreased inflammation (<a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394258">

1342Malfait et al., 2000</a>

1343; <a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394255">

1344Costa et al., 2004a</a>

1345,<a href="#R15" rid="R15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394192">

1346b</a>

1347, <a href="#R16" rid="R16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394244">

13482007</a>

1349). Decreased inflammation and reduction in secretions of pro-inflammatory and matrix-degrading effector molecules by the synovial cell connective tissue membrane lining the joints are important for symptomatic treatment of patients with rheumatoid arthritis. Pro-inflammatory and matrix-degrading effector molecules produced in excess are primary contributors to cartilage degradation over time (<a href="#R46" rid="R46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394234">

1350Ospelt et al., 2004</a>

1351; <a href="#R45" rid="R45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394212">

1352Neumann et al., 2010</a>

1353).</p>

1354<p id="P33" class="p p-last">

1355The improvement of pain scores provided by transdermal CBD is an indirect measure of joint inflammation and direct measure of function. The PWL in response to noxious heat stimuli was optimal with both the 6.2 and 62.3 mg/day doses. These concentrations are similar to optimal doses of intraperitoneally injected or orally administered CBD used to decrease hypersensitivity and inflammation in a collagen/CFA-induced arthritis model in mice (<a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394219">

1356Malfait et al., 2000</a>

1357). Analogous to the results presented here, the highest dose of CBD administered in that study also did not perform as well as the next lower dose. <a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394181">

1358Malfait et al. (2000)</a>

1359 found that intraperitoneal treatments using 5 mg/kg was optimal for relief of arthritis symptoms and 25 mg/kg for orally administered treatments. Orally administered CBD at concentrations of 10&#x02013;40 mg/kg were also able to inhibit thermal hypersensitivity at 3 h post carrageenan-induced inflammation of the hindpaw (<a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394209">

1360Costa et al., 2004a</a>

1361). In the same study at 6 h post inflammation, CBD treatment with two orally administered lower doses, 5 and 7.5 mg/kg, was also effective.</p>

1363<div id="S29" class="sec">

1364<h3 id="S29title">

13654.3 Mechanisms of action</h3>

1366<p id="P34" class="p p-first">

1367Peripheral inflammation and hypersensitivity are reversed by pharmacological inactivation of both central and peripheral neurons and central microglia (<a href="#R55" rid="R55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394222">

1368Sluka et al., 1993</a>

1369, <a href="#R56" rid="R56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394227">

13701994</a>

1371; <a href="#R25" rid="R25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394205">

1372Guo and Schluesener, 2006</a>

1373; <a href="#R50" rid="R50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_710755513">

1374Roberts et al., 2009</a>

1375). Although CBD is described as an attenuator of both mechanical and heat hypersensitivity induced by inflammatory and neuropathic pain models, the exact mechanism of action is as yet unknown (<a href="#R41" rid="R41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394260">

1376Mechoulam and Hanus, 2002</a>

1377; <a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394232">

1378Kress and Kuner, 2009</a>

1379). Unlike THC and related cannabinoids, phytocannabinoid-CBD, an important bioactive component of Cannabis sativa without psychotropic effect, is an antagonist of orphan G protein-coupled receptor 55 (GPR55, a potential third metabotropic cannabinoid receptor) without binding to CB1 and CB2 receptors (<a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394242">

1380Begg et al., 2005</a>

1381; <a href="#R8" rid="R8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394208">

1382Brown, 2007</a>

1383). CBD interacts with GPR55 resulting in inactivation of its pronociceptive signalling (<a href="#R51" rid="R51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394218">

1384Ryberg et al., 2007</a>

1385; <a href="#R58" rid="R58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394201">

1386Staton et al., 2008</a>

1387; <a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394199">

1388Godlewski et al., 2009</a>

1389).</p>

1390<p id="P35">

1391CBD also acts as an agonist for ionotropic cannabinoid receptors including chemo- and thermosensitive members of the TRP channel superfamily (TRPV1, TRPA1, TRPV2, TRPV3, TRPV4) (<a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394254">

1392Fioravanti et al., 2008</a>

1393; <a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394257">

1394Kress and Kuner, 2009</a>

1395). A particular focus has been on TRPA1 and TRPV1, two widely co-expressed ion channels found in CGRP expressing peptidergic nociceptors essential for neurogenic inflammation, oedema formation and inflammation-induced mechanical and thermal hypersensitivity (<a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394196">

1396Davis et al., 2000</a>

1397; <a href="#R60" rid="R60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394253">

1398Szabo et al., 2005</a>

1399; <a href="#R48" rid="R48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394214">

1400Petrus et al., 2007</a>

1401; <a href="#R30" rid="R30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394186">

1402Kerstein et al., 2009</a>

1403; <a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394198">

1404Akopian, 2011</a>

1405). Their activation by CBD <em>

1406in vitro</em>

1407 results in desensitized responses following noxious stimulation with capsaicin or mustard oil, their respective agonists. This mechanism potentially decreases neuropeptide expression (<a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394204">

1408Bisogno et al., 2001</a>

1409; <a href="#R15" rid="R15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394246">

1410Costa et al., 2004b</a>

1411; <a href="#R2" rid="R2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_827220750">

1412Akopian et al., 2008</a>

1413, <a href="#R3" rid="R3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394202">

14142009</a>

1415). <em>

1416In vivo</em>

1417 absence or inhibition of TRPA1 results in reduced mechanical hypersensitivity in animal models of inflammation (<a href="#R48" rid="R48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394259">

1418Petrus et al., 2007</a>

1419; <a href="#R39" rid="R39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394252">

1420McGaraughty et al., 2010</a>

1421; <a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394179">

1422Akopian, 2011</a>

1423). Absence of TRPV1 <em>

1424in vivo</em>

1425 reduces inflammation-induced swelling, thermal hypersensitivity and nociceptive behaviour in various pain models (<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394174">

1426Caterina et al., 2000</a>

1427; <a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394188">

1428Davis et al., 2000</a>

1429; <a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394236">

1430Keeble et al., 2005</a>

1431; <a href="#R60" rid="R60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394233">

1432Szabo et al., 2005</a>

1433). In naive animals, TRPV1 immunoreactivity is localized in nociceptive primary afferents innervating the knee joint. After inflammation, TRPV1 expression increases not only in primary afferents, but is detected in synoviocytes which secrete lubricating fluid into the synovial space and function as local immune cells (<a href="#R31" rid="R31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394224">

1434Kochukov et al., 2009</a>

1435). Once activated, synoviocytes release pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines such as IL1-&#x003b1;, IL-6, TNF&#x003b1; and RANTES, as well as COX-2 (<a href="#R24" rid="R24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394250">

1436Guerne et al., 1989</a>

1437; <a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394180">

1438Berckmans et al., 2005</a>

1439; <a href="#R62" rid="R62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394229">

1440Westlund et al., 2010</a>

1441). Released TNF&#x003b1; not only sensitizes primary afferents (<a href="#R57" rid="R57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394228">

1442Sorkin and Doom, 2000</a>

1443; <a href="#R52" rid="R52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394203">

1444Schafers and Sorkin, 2008</a>

1445), but also acts in an autocrine fashion, increasing the expression of TRPV1 in synoviocytes (<a href="#R31" rid="R31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394183">

1446Kochukov et al., 2009</a>

1447). Primary afferents are thus not only sensitized by peripheral release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, but are surrounded by cells that produce and release these molecules themselves. Release of TNF&#x003b1; from cultured synoviocytes from arthritic animals is reduced by application of CBD (<a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394185">

1448Malfait et al., 2000</a>

1449) as well as by TRPV1 antagonists (<a href="#R31" rid="R31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394190">

1450Kochukov et al., 2009</a>

1451). Desensitization of TRPV1 by CBD could thus interrupt further progression of this cycle. Further studies are needed to identify specific receptors and mechanisms underlying the anti-inflammatory and anti-hyperalgesic effects of CBD.</p>

1452<p id="P36">

1453The pro-inflammatory/pro-pain spiral is further driven by similar mechanisms which induce the expression of pro-inflammatory biomarkers in the DRG of the afferent nerves and centrally in the spinal cord. Transdermal CBD application was successful in decreasing monoarthritis-associated increases of pro-inflammatory biomarkers in neuronal tissues. Here, adjuvant-induced monoarthritis led to significant increases of TNF&#x003b1; expression in DRG which was decreased to baseline levels by transdermal CBD application.</p>

1454<p id="P37">

1455In this study, the expression of CGRP in spinal cord was increased after peripheral inflammation as previously reported (<a href="#R54" rid="R54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394207">

1456Sluka and Westlund, 1993</a>

1457), and was decreased by high doses (6.2 and 62 mg/day) of transdermal CBD. Like other neuropeptides, CGRP is rapidly transported to nerve terminals for release centrally as well as peripherally where as a potent vasodilator it contributes to neurogenic inflammation (<a href="#R28" rid="R28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394245">

1458Kawasaki et al., 1988</a>

1459; <a href="#R64" rid="R64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394223">

1460Zhang et al., 2001</a>

1461). Although increases in CGRP are described in DRG after hindpaw inflammation (<a href="#R44" rid="R44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394248">

1462Nahin and Byers, 1994</a>

1463), no significant changes in DRG expression were observed here (data not shown), possibly due to the small number of sensory neurons innervating the joint. It is also plausible that by 7 days post monoarthritis induction, neuropeptide content has stabilized.</p>

1464<p id="P38" class="p p-last">

1465In monoarthritic animals, spinal cord OX42 expression is increased in activated microglia, the immune cells specific to the central nervous system, as previously described for CFA-induced ankle inflammation and trinitrobenzene sulfonic acid (TNBS)-induced pancreatitis (<a href="#R53" rid="R53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394191">

1466Shan et al., 2007</a>

1467; <a href="#R34" rid="R34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394261">

1468Liu et al., 2012</a>

1469). Treatment with high doses of transdermal CBD in this study effectively reduced OX42 expression below baseline levels, indicating reduced microglial activation. CBD also reduced microglial/macrophage spinal cord expression in a mouse encephalopathy model (<a href="#R32" rid="R32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394235">

1470Kozela et al., 2011</a>

1471).</p>

1472</div>

1473<div id="S30" class="sec sec-last">

1474<h3 id="S30title">

14754.4 Absence of psychoactive behaviours</h3>

1476<p id="P39" class="p p-first-last">

1477CBD is a known non-psychoactive cannabinoid, and due to its low affinity for the CB1 receptor it would be expected that exploratory behavioural activity would be similar among treatment groups compared to negative side-effects associated with THC (<a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394240">

1478Croxford, 2003</a>

1479; <a href="#R37" rid="R37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394213">

1480Malone et al., 2009</a>

1481). This was demonstrated in this study by lack of CBD-induced changes in open-field exploration among naive treatment groups. Combinatorial with psychoactivity, side-effects such as hypothermia and hypomobility induced by THC are avoided with use of CBD (<a href="#R67" rid="R67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394178">

1482Zimmer et al., 1999</a>

1483).</p>

1487<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S31title">

14885. Conclusion</h2>

1489<p id="P40" class="p p-first-last">

1490These studies demonstrate transdermal administration of CBD has long-lasting therapeutic effects without psychoactive side-effects. Thus, use of topical CBD has potential as effective treatment of arthritic symptomatology. At present, one in five (21%) adults worldwide are diagnosed with some form of arthritis by their physicians (<a href="#R27" rid="R27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534394189">

1491Helmick et al., 2008</a>

1492). The data presented suggest transdermal CBD is a good candidate for developing improved therapies for these debilitating disease.</p>
