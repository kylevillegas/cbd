824<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm139966721231632title">

825Abstract</h2>

826<!--article-meta-->

827<div>

828<p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">

829Certain types of nonpsychoactive cannabinoids can potentiate glycine receptors (GlyRs), an important target for nociceptive regulation at the spinal level. However, little is known about the potential and mechanism of glycinergic cannabinoids for chronic pain treatment. We report that systemic and intrathecal administration of cannabidiol (CBD), a major nonpsychoactive component of marijuana, and its modified derivatives significantly suppress chronic inflammatory and neuropathic pain without causing apparent analgesic tolerance in rodents. The cannabinoids significantly potentiate glycine currents in dorsal horn neurons in rat spinal cord slices. The analgesic potency of 11 structurally similar cannabinoids is positively correlated with cannabinoid potentiation of the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs. In contrast, the cannabinoid analgesia is neither correlated with their binding affinity for CB1 and CB2 receptors nor with their psychoactive side effects. NMR analysis reveals a direct interaction between CBD and S296 in the third transmembrane domain of purified &#x003b1;3 GlyR. The cannabinoid-induced analgesic effect is absent in mice lacking the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs. Our findings suggest that the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs mediate glycinergic cannabinoid-induced suppression of chronic pain. These cannabinoids may represent a novel class of therapeutic agents for the treatment of chronic pain and other diseases involving GlyR dysfunction.</p>

830</div>

831</div>

832<div id="idm139966721231008" class="tsec sec">

833<h2 class="headless nomenu">

834</h2>

835<p id="__p3" class="p p-first">

836Chronic pain, particularly neuropathic pain, is a major clinical problem that is difficult to treat (<a href="#bib61" rid="bib61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400436">

837Zhuo, 2007</a>

838). Despite an intensive search for new analgesics in the last several decades, the need for novel therapeutic strategies remains unmet because virtually every blockbuster drug for the treatment of chronic pain produces aversive side effects (<a href="#bib38" rid="bib38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400386">

839Mogil, 2009</a>

840; <a href="#bib22" rid="bib22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400394">

841Harrison, 2011</a>

842). Marijuana has been used to treat chronic pain for thousands of years (<a href="#bib7" rid="bib7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400419">

843Burns and Ineck, 2006</a>

844; <a href="#bib41" rid="bib41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400429">

845Murray et al., 2007</a>

846). However, the widespread use of medical marijuana is still controversial because the plant produces both therapeutic and psychoactive effects. Marijuana consists of &#x0223c;400 chemical compounds, and &#x0223c;60 of them are structurally related cannabinoids. &#x00394;<sup>

8479</sup>

848-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and cannabidiol (CBD) among cannabinoids are major psychoactive and nonpsychoactive components of marijuana, respectively (<a href="#bib27" rid="bib27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400406">

849Howlett et al., 2002</a>

850; <a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400397">

851Costa, 2007</a>

852). There is strong evidence suggesting that nonpsychoactive cannabinoids can also alleviate chronic inflammatory and neuropathic pain in animals (<a href="#bib12" rid="bib12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400424">

853Costa et al., 2007</a>

854; <a href="#bib30" rid="bib30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400367">

855Izzo et al., 2009</a>

856). Several recent clinical studies have demonstrated that combination of THC and CBD can be an effective therapeutic option for patients with neuropathic pain and other types of chronic pain (<a href="#bib42" rid="bib42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400365">

857Nurmikko et al., 2007</a>

858; <a href="#bib48" rid="bib48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400445">

859Turcotte et al., 2010</a>

860; <a href="#bib34" rid="bib34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400413">

861Lynch and Campbell, 2011</a>

862). However, there is a need to improve the efficacy and tolerability of these agents in treating chronic pain. One primary obstacle to development of these agents is the uncertainty about the molecular targets for cannabinoid-induced analgesic effects. For instance, the role of spinal CB1 receptors (CB1Rs) in the pain process is debatable. Some studies suggest that activation of CB1Rs in the spinal dorsal horn can facilitate pain (<a href="#bib45" rid="bib45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400447">

863Pern&#x000ed;a-Andrade et al., 2009</a>

864; <a href="#bib59" rid="bib59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400415">

865Zhang et al., 2010</a>

866; <a href="#bib58" rid="bib58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400434">

867Zeilhofer et al., 2012</a>

868). Notably, THC-induced analgesia in the tail flick reflex, a test for nociceptive pain threshold, remains intact in mice devoid of CB1 receptors (CB1<sup>

869&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup>

870; <a href="#bib62" rid="bib62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400405">

871Zimmer et al., 1999</a>

872; <a href="#bib27" rid="bib27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400407">

873Howlett et al., 2002</a>

874).</p>

875<p id="__p4">

876Recent studies have shown that glycine receptors (GlyRs) are an important target for cannabinoids in the central nervous system. For instance, several synthetic and phytocannabinoids, including THC and CBD, can potentiate glycine currents (I<sub>

877Gly</sub>

878) in native neurons isolated from the ventral tegmental area, amygdala, hippocampus, and spinal cord and in various heterologous cells expressing recombinant GlyRs (<a href="#bib25" rid="bib25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400437">

879Hejazi et al., 2006</a>

880; <a href="#bib54" rid="bib54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400440">

881Yang et al., 2008</a>

882; <a href="#bib2" rid="bib2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400414">

883Ahrens et al., 2009a</a>

884,<a href="#bib3" rid="bib3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400451">

885b</a>

886; <a href="#bib14" rid="bib14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400375">

887Demir et al., 2009</a>

888; <a href="#bib18" rid="bib18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400421">

889Foadi et al., 2010</a>

890; <a href="#bib52" rid="bib52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400417">

891Xiong et al., 2011</a>

892, <a href="#bib53" rid="bib53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400366">

8932012</a>

894; <a href="#bib55" rid="bib55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400363">

895Yevenes and Zeilhofer, 2011a</a>

896,<a href="#bib56" rid="bib56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_311525675">

897Yevenes and Zeilhofer, 2011b</a>

898). GlyRs are thought to play an important role in the antinociceptive process (<a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400377">

899Harvey et al., 2004</a>

900, <a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400438">

9012009</a>

902; <a href="#bib57" rid="bib57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400403">

903Zeilhofer, 2005</a>

904; <a href="#bib33" rid="bib33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_311525676">

905Lynch and Callister, 2006</a>

906; <a href="#bib45" rid="bib45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400402">

907Pern&#x000ed;a-Andrade et al., 2009</a>

908; <a href="#bib58" rid="bib58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400450">

909Zeilhofer et al., 2012</a>

910). There are four isoforms of the &#x003b1; subunits (&#x003b1;1&#x02013;4) and a single isoform of the &#x003b2; subunit. The adult form of GlyRs are composed of &#x003b1; and &#x003b2; subunits in a pentameric assembly (<a href="#bib32" rid="bib32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400420">

911Lynch, 2004</a>

912). The role of the &#x003b1;3 subunit in modulating inflammatory pain has been the focus of many discussions. The &#x003b1;3-containing GlyRs are abundantly located in the lamina II of the spinal dorsal horn, an area known for integrating nociceptive information. Experimental evidence suggests that prostaglandin E<sub>

9132</sub>

914 (PGE<sub>

9152</sub>

916), a critical mediator of central and peripheral pain sensitization, selectively inhibits the &#x003b1;3 GlyR function (<a href="#bib1" rid="bib1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400452">

917Ahmadi et al., 2002</a>

918; <a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400427">

919Harvey et al., 2004</a>

920, <a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400411">

9212009</a>

922). Such disinhibition of the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs is found to contribute to the mechanism of chronic inflammatory pain induced by the intraplantar injection of CFA (<a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400370">

923Harvey et al., 2004</a>

924, <a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400382">

9252009</a>

926).</p>

927<p id="__p5" class="p">

928Our recent study suggests that cannabinoid potentiation of GlyRs can produce a potent analgesic effect in mice (<a href="#bib52" rid="bib52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400426">

929Xiong et al., 2011</a>

930). The idea was mainly based on the results obtained in the tail flick test, a measure of transient nociception which only resembles the normal physiological state (<a href="#bib19" rid="bib19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400439">

931Grossman et al., 1982</a>

932). It is important to determine whether allosteric facilitation of GlyRs by cannabinoids contributes to the treatment of pathological or chronic pain states. Here, we demonstrate that glycinergic cannabinoids suppress inflammatory and neuropathic pain without significantly causing major psychoactive side effect and analgesic tolerance. The suppression of pathological pain by glycinergic cannabinoids is mediated through an &#x003b1;3 GlyR-dependent mechanism. We also provide mechanistic details of drug&#x02013;receptor interaction and strategies for future studies to develop a new generation of glycinergic cannabinoid-based agents for the treatment of chronic pain and other diseases involving GlyR dysfunction.</p>

1195Patapoutian et al., 2009</a>

1199Harvey et al., 2004</a>

12012</sub>

1202 receptors (EP2Rs). To address this question, we coexpressed the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs with EP2Rs in HEK 293 cells. In these cells, preincubation with 10 &#x000b5;M PGE<sub>

12032</sub>

1204 significantly reduced the amplitude of current activated by 200 &#x000b5;M (EC<sub>

120520</sub>

1206) glycine (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3371734/figure/fig4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig4" rid-ob="ob-fig4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig4">

1207<span>

1209</a>

1210). In contrast, PGE<sub>

12112</sub>

1212 did not significantly alter I<sub>

1213Gly</sub>

1214 in cells coexpressing EP2Rs and &#x003b1;1 GlyRs. These findings are consistent with the observations described in a previous study (<a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400446">

1215Harvey et al., 2004</a>

1216). The PGE<sub>

12172</sub>

1218 inhibition of the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs developed slowly and reached the maximal 5&#x02013;8 min after application of PGE<sub>

12192</sub>

1220 (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3371734/figure/fig4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig4" rid-ob="ob-fig4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig4">

1221<span>

1742<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec10title">

1743DISCUSSION</h2>

1744<p id="__p28" class="p p-first">

1745Spinal &#x003b1;3 GlyRs have been proposed as an important target for pain treatment. However, the &#x003b1;3 GlyR-based therapeutic agents in the treatment of chronic pain or other diseases are not yet available. The current study has provided several lines of evidence to suggest that CBD and DH-CBD suppress persistent inflammatory and neuropathic pain by targeting the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs in rodents. Consistent with in vitro observation that DH-CBD was more efficacious than CBD in potentiating I<sub>

1746Gly</sub>

1747, DH-CBD was more potent than CBD in reducing chronic pain. DH-CBD also attenuated i.t. PGE<sub>

17482</sub>

1749-induced persistent pain hypersensitivity in mice. Several lines of evidence suggest that cannabinoid-induced analgesia is mediated through the &#x003b1;3 GlyR-dependent pathway. First, both CBD and DH-CBD&#x02013;induced analgesic effects were significantly reduced in mice lacking the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs but not in mice lacking the CB1 and CB2 receptors. Second, DD-CBD inhibited DH-CBD&#x02013;induced potentiation of the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs and analgesic effect in chronic pain. Third, structural and functional analysis reveals that the magnitude of the cannabinoid-induced analgesic effect was correlated with cannabinoid potentiation of the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs but not with the cannabinoid binding affinity for CB1 and CB2 receptors. Because of substantially reduced CB1 binding affinity, DH-CBD, even at high concentrations (50 mg/kg i.p.), did not produce the psychoactive effects commonly associated with cannabinoid activation of CB1 receptors. Collectively, glycinergic cannabinoids represent a new class of therapeutic agents that selectively relieve pathological pain by targeting the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs.</p>

1750<p id="__p29">

1751The data from NMR titration and NOESY experiments strongly indicates the direct interaction of CBD with residue S296. The change in NMR signal intensity upon CBD titration suggests that the protein motion at S296 is sensitive to CBD binding. The molecular model of the &#x003b1;3 GlyR TM domains reveals that S296 is located near the intracellular end of the TM3 helix, with its side chain facing the lipids. Direct interaction of CBD with &#x003b1;3 GlyR-TM protein was confirmed by the intermolecular NOESY cross peaks between CBD and the protein. There is a transition from the free to the CBD-bound state as indicated by the observation that a free S296 resonance and a bound resonance appeared sequentially with an intermediate coexistence of both peaks. This finding also favors a protein conformational change at S296 in the presence of CBD.</p>

1752<p id="__p30">

1753Our molecular dynamics simulations suggest that CBD-&#x003b1;3 GlyR binding interactions involve the S296 residue of the &#x003b1;3 GlyR TM domain on the principal side, and the lipid molecules on the complementary side. The molecular docking analyses reveal that the binding free energies of the potentiator CBD and the inhibitor DD-CBD at this protein-lipid interfacial site are very similar, suggesting that the binding affinities are within the same order of magnitude. This finding favors the idea that the DD-CBD inhibition of CBD potentiation of I<sub>

1754Gly</sub>

1755 is through a competitive mechanism by acting near or at the same site involving S296. It should be pointed out that both DH-CBD and DD-CBD are modulators of GlyRs. Unlike orthosteric ligands, these modulators bind to allosteric sites. The molecular nature of competitiveness of these modulators remains unknown. Although the data from the in vitro study together with the result of molecular dynamics simulations suggest that DD-CBD may compete for the same site with CBD, our experimental data alone are not sufficient to conclude that DD-CBD acts as a competitive antagonist of CBD. Future studies should be performed to examine the effect of DD-CBD on the purified &#x003b1;1 or &#x003b1;3 GlyR-TM proteins using NMR analysis.</p>

1756<p id="__p31">

1757The kinetic analysis suggests that DH-CBD increases the agonist binding affinity of GlyRs. DH-CBD accelerated receptor activation rate and, on the other side, slowed receptor deactivation rate. Although receptor activation time represents agonist binding and/or channel gating, receptor deactivation time reflects the kinetics of agonist unbinding/channel closing or combination of two. The DH-CBD&#x02013;induced changes in GlyR kinetics appears relevant to DH-CBD&#x02013;induced potentiation of I<sub>

1758Gly</sub>

1759 because both S296A and DD-CBD, which inhibited DH-CBD potentiation of I<sub>

1760Gly</sub>

1761, abolished DH-CBD alteration of receptor gating kinetics. DH-CBD shifted in a parallel manner the glycine-concentration response curve to the left, favoring a hypothesis that DH-CBD allosterically increases the agonist binding affinity of GlyRs. However, this notion should be made with caution, as slow deactivation time of GlyRs could reflect a slow channel closing, unbinding rate, or both in the presence of DH-CBD. In this scenario, slow deactivation time could be a result of slow wash time of DH-CBD because of its hydrophobic nature. One can also argue that the deactivation rate could be contaminated with desensitization rate. However, it is unlikely to be the case in our study because DH-CBD did not significantly affect GlyR desensitization rate while slowing deactivation.</p>

1762<p id="__p32">

1763Several preclinical persistent and chronic pain models were tested in this study. Intraplantar CFA injection has been widely used as an inflammatory pain model. Both systemic and i.t. injection of DH-CBD significantly reduced mechanical pain hypersensitivity induced by CFA. PGE<sub>

17642</sub>

1765 is one of the major proinflammatory substances that promote nociceptive processing in the spinal cord and peripheral tissues upon various noxious stimuli such as CFA (<a href="#bib49" rid="bib49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400428">

1766Vanegas and Schaible, 2001</a>

1767; <a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400368">

1768Harvey et al., 2004</a>

1769, <a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400396">

17702009</a>

1771; <a href="#bib58" rid="bib58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400453">

1772Zeilhofer et al., 2012</a>

1773). Consistent with the observations in CFA-induced inflammatory pain models, DH-CBD also produced an analgesic effect in i.t. injection of PGE<sub>

17742</sub>

1775-induced nociception in mice. More importantly, i.t. application of DH-CBD exerted potent inhibition of chronic neuropathic pain in rats. Neuropathic pain is a substantial health issue because currently available therapies are far from satisfactory. Although neuropathic pain and inflammatory pain differ in pathogenesis, molecular mechanisms, and treatments, these two types of persistent pain may be modulated by similar synaptic mechanism at the spinal level (<a href="#bib58" rid="bib58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400412">

1776Zeilhofer et al., 2012</a>

1777). The data presented in our study suggest that the &#x003b1;3 GlyR contributes to the mechanisms that modulate both types of pain. Yet, we cannot exclude the potential involvement of other subtypes of GlyRs in the pain modulation. In addition to reducing chronic pain, DH-CBD can also attenuate acute pain. DH-CBD increased the time latency in the tail flick reflex in mice (<a href="#bib52" rid="bib52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400378">

1778Xiong et al., 2011</a>

1779). This analgesic effect induced by DH-CBD was abolished in mice depleted with the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs but remained intact in mice depleted with the CB1 receptors. Consistent with this idea, i.t. application of DH-CBD at higher doses also significantly increased the contralateral PWL from baseline in both inflammatory and neuropathic pain in rats.</p>

1780<p id="__p33">

1781i.t. application of DH-CBD seems the most efficacious way to suppress mechanical and thermal pain hypersensitivity in both inflammatory and neuropathic pain conditions. This idea is consistent with the distinct distribution pattern of the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs in lamina II of the spinal dorsal horn (<a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400373">

1782Harvey et al., 2004</a>

1783). Moreover, the &#x003b1;3 GlyRs are either absent or less expressed in primary sensory neurons such as dorsal root ganglion neurons (<a href="#bib32" rid="bib32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400448">

1784Lynch, 2004</a>

1785). Oral administration of cannabinoids is not an ideal route for drug delivery because primary cannabinoids are largely metabolized by the liver (<a href="#bib28" rid="bib28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_484120421">

1786Huestis and Pertwee, 2005</a>

1787). It is worth mentioning that one of the common practices to deliver medical cannabis to humans is via sublingual spray, which bypasses the liver and delivers drugs directly into the blood stream (<a href="#bib42" rid="bib42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_290400385">

1788Nurmikko et al., 2007</a>

1789). Collectively, we propose that i.t. injection of cannabinoids should be the most efficacious route to treat patients with chronic neuropathic pain.</p>

1790<p id="__p34">

1791Among 11 cannabinoid analogues evaluated in this study, DH-CBD has emerged as an ideal glycinergic cannabinoid that can be used to treat chronic pain without causing aversive effects. Unlike some of the analogues that not only showed relatively high efficacy in potentiating I<sub>

1792Gly</sub>

1793 but also demonstrated relatively high affinity to bind to CB1 receptors, DH-CBD displayed a low affinity for CB1 receptors and at the same time is one of the most efficacious positive modulators of GlyRs. It has been shown consistently in our correlation analysis that most psychoactive effects induced by cannabinoids are associated with CB1 receptor binding affinity but not cannabinoid-induced potentiation of GlyRs. Conversely, the cannabinoid-induced analgesic effect in chronic pain is correlated with cannabinoid potentiation of GlyRs but not with cannabinoid binding affinity to CB1 receptors. These principles may apply to future studies in developing a new generation of glycinergic cannabinoids in the treatment of chronic pain. In addition to lacking a psychoactive side effect, glycinergic cannabinoids are unlikely to develop drug tachyphylaxis or tolerance, one of the major barriers for long-term pain management with currently available clinical agents. Repeated application of DH-CBD either i.p. or i.t. exhibited similar analgesic potency in both inflammatory and neuropathic pain. This finding is not unexpected because glycinergic cannabinoids act on the GlyRs as allosteric modulators instead of agonists or antagonists.</p>

1794<p id="__p35" class="p p-last">

1795Collectively, we have provided evidence to suggest that glycinergic cannabinoids are ideal therapeutic agents in the treatment of inflammatory and neuropathic pain. They can effectively attenuate pathological pain without significantly causing major psychoactive side effect and analgesic tolerance. The mechanistic details of drug&#x02013;receptor interaction could help to develop novel agents for the treatment of painful conditions and other diseases involving GlyR impairment.</p>

2061Whole-cell kinetic measurements. </h4>

2062<p id="__p53" class="p p-first-last">

2063Receptor activation rate for current induced by 1 mM glycine was estimated by measuring the slope of the initial inward component of current between 10 and 90% of the maximal current (10&#x02013;90% rise time). The deactivation time was recorded for 30 s immediately after 10 ms application of 1 mM glycine. The time constants of deactivation and desensitization were determined by fitting with mono-exponential functions using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm in Clampfit 9.2 (Molecular Devices).</p>
