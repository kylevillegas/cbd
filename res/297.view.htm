898<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm139944688469760title">

899Abstract</h2>

900<!--article-meta-->

901<div>

902<p id="__p1" class="p p-first-last">

903Generalized Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD) is one of the most common anxiety conditions with impairment in social life. Cannabidiol (CBD), one major non-psychotomimetic compound of the <em>

904cannabis sativa</em>

905 plant, has shown anxiolytic effects both in humans and in animals. This preliminary study aimed to compare the effects of a simulation public speaking test (SPST) on healthy control (HC) patients and treatment-na&#x000ef;ve SAD patients who received a single dose of CBD or placebo. A total of 24 never-treated patients with SAD were allocated to receive either CBD (600&#x02009;mg; <em>

906n</em>

907=12) or placebo (placebo; <em>

908n</em>

909=12) in a double-blind randomized design 1&#x02009;h and a half before the test. The same number of HC (<em>

910n</em>

911=12) performed the SPST without receiving any medication. Each volunteer participated in only one experimental session in a double-blind procedure. Subjective ratings on the Visual Analogue Mood Scale (VAMS) and Negative Self-Statement scale (SSPS-N) and physiological measures (blood pressure, heart rate, and skin conductance) were measured at six different time points during the SPST. The results were submitted to a repeated-measures analysis of variance. Pretreatment with CBD significantly reduced anxiety, cognitive impairment and discomfort in their speech performance, and significantly decreased alert in their anticipatory speech. The placebo group presented higher anxiety, cognitive impairment, discomfort, and alert levels when compared with the control group as assessed with the VAMS. The SSPS-N scores evidenced significant increases during the testing of placebo group that was almost abolished in the CBD group. No significant differences were observed between CBD and HC in SSPS-N scores or in the cognitive impairment, discomfort, and alert factors of VAMS. The increase in anxiety induced by the SPST on subjects with SAD was reduced with the use of CBD, resulting in a similar response as the HC.</p>

921<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">

922INTRODUCTION</h2>

923<p id="__p2" class="p p-first">

924Generalized Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD) is one of the most common anxiety conditions and is associated with impairment in social adjustment to the usual aspects of daily life, increased disability, dysfunction, and a loss of productivity (<a href="#bib30" rid="bib30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520897">

925Kessler, 2007</a>

926; <a href="#bib22" rid="bib22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659048">

927Filho <em>

928et al</em>

929, 2010</a>

930). SAD tends to follow a long-term and unremitting course and is rarely resolved without treatment (<a href="#bib14" rid="bib14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659094">

931Crippa <em>

932et al</em>

933, 2007</a>

934; <a href="#bib9" rid="bib9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659074">

935Chagas <em>

936et al</em>

937, 2010</a>

938).</p>

939<p id="__p3">

940The pharmacological management of SAD remains problematic, despite several guidelines or consensus statements issued over the past few years (<a href="#bib8" rid="bib8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520892">

941Canadian Psychiatric Association, 2006</a>

942; <a href="#bib32" rid="bib32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659098">

943Montgomery <em>

944et al</em>

945, 2004</a>

946). As this anxiety disorder is often poorly controlled by the currently available drugs (only about 30&#x00025; of the subjects achieve true recovery or remission without residual symptomatology (<a href="#bib3" rid="bib3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659093">

947Blanco <em>

948et al</em>

949, 2002</a>

950)), there is a clear need to search for novel therapeutic agents.</p>

951<p id="__p4">

952Subjects with SAD seem to be more likely to use <em>

953cannabis sativa</em>

954 (<em>

955cannabis</em>

956) than those without other anxiety disorders to &#x02018;self-medicate' anxiety reactions (<a href="#bib6" rid="bib6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659067">

957Buckner <em>

958et al</em>

959, 2008</a>

960). However, the relationship of <em>

961cannabis</em>

962 with anxiety is paradoxical. <em>

963Cannabis</em>

964 users reported the reduction of anxiety as one of the motivations for its use; on the other hand, episodes of intense anxiety or panic are among the most common undesirable effects of the drug (<a href="#bib18" rid="bib18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659064">

965Crippa <em>

966et al</em>

967, 2009</a>

968). These apparently conflicting statements may partly reflect the fact that low doses of the best-known constituent of the plant, &#x00394;9-tetrahydrocannabinol (&#x00394;9-THC), engender anxiolytic-like effects, whereas higher doses produce anxiogenic reactions (<a href="#bib18" rid="bib18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659050">

969Crippa <em>

970et al</em>

971, 2009</a>

972).</p>

973<p id="__p5">

974Moreover, other components of the plant can influence its pharmacological activity; in particular, cannabidiol (CBD), one major non-psychotomimetic compound of the plant, has psychological effects substantially different from those of 9-THC (<a href="#bib42" rid="bib42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659089">

975Zuardi, 2008</a>

976). Oral administration of CBD to healthy volunteers has been shown to attenuate the anxiogenic effect of &#x00394;9-THC and does not seem to involve any pharmacokinetic interactions (<a href="#bib45" rid="bib45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659045">

977Zuardi <em>

978et al</em>

979, 1982</a>

980). In animal studies, CBD has similar effects to anxiolytic drugs in different paradigms including conditioned emotional response, the Vogel conflict test, and the elevated plus-maze test (<a href="#bib42" rid="bib42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659097">

981Zuardi, 2008</a>

982). In human studies, the anxiolytic effects of CBD have been elicited in subjects submitted to the Simulation Public Speaking Test (SPST) (<a href="#bib43" rid="bib43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520891">

983Zuardi <em>

984et al</em>

985, 1993</a>

986). Using functional neuroimaging in healthy volunteers, we have observed that CBD has anxiolytic properties and that these effects are associated with an action on the limbic and paralimbic brain areas (<a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659058">

987Fusar-Poli <em>

988et al</em>

989, 2009a</a>

990; <a href="#bib16" rid="bib16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659049">

991Crippa <em>

992et al</em>

993, 2004</a>

994).</p>

995<p id="__p6">

996Recently, we investigated the central effects of CBD on regional cerebral blood flow (rCBF), using single photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) in patients with SAD. Relative to placebo, CBD was associated with significant decreases in subjective anxiety induced by the SPECT procedure and modulated the same brain areas as the healthy volunteers (<a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659051">

997Crippa <em>

998et al</em>

999, 2010</a>

1000, <a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659071">

10012011</a>

1002).</p>

1003<p id="__p7" class="p p-last">

1004The data reviewed above led to the hypothesis that CBD may be an effective compound in the treatment of SAD symptoms. As a first step to investigate this hypothesis, we used the SPST, an experimental model for the induction of anxiety. SPST has apparent and predictive validity for SAD because the fear of speaking in public is a cardinal manifestation of SAD, and there is pharmacological evidence that the response pattern to some substances in the SPST is similar to the clinical response presented by patients with SAD (<a href="#bib26" rid="bib26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659056">

1005Graeff <em>

1006et al</em>

1007, 2003</a>

1008; <a href="#bib5" rid="bib5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659072">

1009Brunello <em>

1010et al</em>

1011, 2000</a>

1012). In this preliminary study, we aimed to measure the subjective and physiological effects of SPST on healthy control (HC) and on treatment-na&#x000ef;ve SAD patients, who received a single dose of CBD or placebo, in a double-blind design. We have decided to use a single dose of CBD because of ethical and economical constraints, as a first step in the investigation of a possible anxiolytic action of this cannabinoid in patients with pathological anxiety. For instance, it is important to confirm whether CBD has the advantage of a rapid onset of action, making it particularly suitable for individuals who have episodic performance-related social phobia and who are able to predict the need for treatment well in advance. Considering previous results from a single dose of CBD, it is expected that this cannabinoid will reduce the level of fear provoked by the SPST.</p>

1017<div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-first">

1018<h3 id="__sec3title">

1019Subjects</h3>

1020<p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">

1021A total of 24 subjects with generalized SAD and 12 HC subjects were selected by the screening procedure described below (see section). The SAD patients were randomly assigned to the two groups with 12 subjects each to receive CBD (600&#x02009;mg&#x02014;SAD-CBD) or placebo (SAD-PLAC), in a double-blind study design. To ensure the adequacy of the matching procedure, the first participant had his treatment blindly chosen between the two treatment options available; the next participant (whose characteristics were matched to the first one's) had his treatment drawn from the remaining option. An equal number of healthy controls (<em>

1022n</em>

1023=12) performed the test without receiving any medication (HC). The groups were matched according to gender, age, years of education, and socioeconomic status. Moreover, the two SAD groups were balanced according to the Social Phobia Inventory (SPIN (<a href="#bib10" rid="bib10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659082">

1024Connor <em>

1025et al</em>

1026, 2000</a>

1027)). All participants were treatment-na&#x000ef;ve (either with pharmacotherapy or psychotherapy) and did not present any other concomitant psychiatric disorder. No subject had a history of head trauma, neurological illness, ECT, substance abuse, or major medical illnesses, based on a semi-standardized medical questionnaire and physical examination. They were all non-smokers (of tobacco) and had not taken any medications for at least 3 months before the study. None of the subject had used marijuana more than five times in their lives (no use in the last year) and none had ever used any other illegal drug. All subjects gave written informed consent after being fully informed about the research procedure, following approval by the local ethical committee (HCRP No. 12407/2009).</p>

1030<h3 id="__sec4title">

1031Screening Procedure and Clinical Assessment</h3>

1032<p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">

1039et al</em>

1040, 2001</a>

1065CBD Preparation</h3>

1066<p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">

1072Zuardi <em>

1073et al</em>

1074, 1993</a>

1075). The same amount of corn oil was used as placebo. The drug and placebo were packed inside identical gelatin capsules. We have chosen the dose of 600&#x02009;mg based on the fact that acute anxiolytic effects of CBD have been observed in healthy controls with doses ranging from 300 (<a href="#bib43" rid="bib43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520898">

10842009b</a>

Therefore, we have decided to use the highest dose of CBD previously found to have anxiolytic effects. The time of assessment after the procedure was chosen based on previous studies that showed that the plasma peak of an oral dose of CBD usually occurs 1&#x02013;2&#x02009;h after ingestion (<a href="#bib1" rid="bib1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659063">

1110<h3 id="__sec6title">

1111Psychological Measurements</h3>

1112<p id="__p11" class="p p-first">

1113The state-anxiety level and other subjective states were evaluated during the test through the Visual Analogue Mood Scale&#x02014;VAMS (<a href="#bib34" rid="bib34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659062">

1114Norris, 1971</a>

1115), translated into Portuguese (<a href="#bib44" rid="bib44" class=" bibr popnode">

1116Zuardi and Karniol, 1981</a>

1117). In this scale, the subject is told to mark a point that identifies his/her present subjective state on a 100-mm straight line placed between two words that describe opposite mood states. VAMS contains 16 items that Norris grouped into four factors. A factorial analysis performed with the Portuguese version of the VAMS also yielded four factors with similar item composition (<a href="#bib43" rid="bib43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520895">

1118Zuardi <em>

1119et al</em>

1120, 1993</a>

1121). The original name of the anxiety factor was preserved, but the names of the remaining factors have been changed to fit the meaning of the items with the highest loads in that particular factor. Thus, the present factors are: (1) anxiety, comprising the items <em>

1122calm&#x02013;excited</em>

1123, <em>

1124relaxed&#x02013;tense</em>

1125, and <em>

1126tranquil&#x02013;troubled</em>

1127; (2) sedation (former mental sedation), including the items <em>

1128alert&#x02013;drowsy</em>

1129, and <em>

1130attentive&#x02013;dreamy</em>

1131; (3) cognitive impairment (former physical sedation), including <em>

1132quick-witted&#x02013;mentally slow, proficient&#x02013;incompetent, energetic&#x02013;lethargic, clear-headed&#x02013;muzzy, gregarious&#x02013;withdrawn, well-coordinated&#x02013;clumsy</em>

1133, and <em>

1134strong&#x02013;feeble</em>

1135; and (4) discomfort (former other feelings and attitudes), made of the items <em>

1136interested&#x02013;bored, happy&#x02013;sad, contented&#x02013;discontented</em>

1137, and <em>

1138amicable&#x02013;antagonistic</em>

1139 (<a href="#bib36" rid="bib36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659076">

1140Parente <em>

1141et al</em>

1142, 2005</a>

1143).</p>

1144<p id="__p12">

1145The Self-Statements during Public Speaking Scale (<a href="#bib29" rid="bib29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659054">

1146Hoffmann and Di Bartolo, 2000</a>

1147) (SSPS), translated into Portuguese (<a href="#bib20" rid="bib20" class=" bibr popnode">

1148de Lima Os&#x000f3;rio <em>

1149et al</em>

1150, 2008</a>

1151) is a self-report instrument that aims to measure the self-perception of performance in the specific situation of public speaking. It is based upon cognitive theories that propose that social anxiety is the result of a negative perception of oneself and of others towards oneself. The scale is comprised of 10 items, rated on a <em>

1152likert</em>

1153 scale from 0 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree), which are organized into two subscales of five items each, for positive or negative self-evaluation. In this study, we applied the negative self-evaluation subscale (SSPS-N).</p>

1154<p id="__p13" class="p p-last">

1155The Bodily Symptoms Scale (BSS) was designed to detect physical symptoms that can, indirectly, influence anxiety measures (<a href="#bib43" rid="bib43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520894">

1156Zuardi <em>

1157et al</em>

1158, 1993</a>

1159). It is organized into 21 items, and the intensity of each symptom is rated from 0 (no symptom) to 5 (highest).</p>

1353Statistical Analysis</h3>

1354<p id="__p19" class="p p-first">

1355Clinical and demographical characteristics were analyzed with the non-parametric tests (gender and socioeconomic level) and by the analysis of variance for one factor (ANOVA), followed by post-hoc Bonferroni's test for multiple comparisons (age, age of SAD onset and SPIN).</p>

1356<p id="__p20">

1357Scores of VAMS's factors, SSPS-N, BSS, arterial diastolic and systolic pressure, heart rate, as well as the SCL and the total number of SF, were transformed by calculating the difference between the score in each phase and the pretest score in the same volunteer. For the analysis, SCL values were converted into natural logarithms (logn). These delta scores were submitted to a repeated-measures analysis of variance (repeated-measures ANOVA), analyzing the factors of phases, groups, and phases by groups' interaction. In the case where sphericity conditions were not reached, the degrees of freedom of the repeated factor were corrected with the Huynh&#x02013;Feldt epsilon. Whenever a significant phase by group interaction occurred, comparisons among the groups were made at each phase using a one-factor ANOVA followed by multiple comparisons with the Bonferroni's test.</p>

1358<p id="__p21" class="p p-last">

1359Data analysis was performed using the SPSS-17 program, and the significance level adopted was <em>

1360p</em>

1361&#x0003c;0.05.</p>

1776<h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec17title">

1777DISCUSSION</h2>

1778<p id="__p29" class="p p-first">

1779As observed in another study of SAD patients' performance on SPST (<a href="#bib15" rid="bib15" class=" bibr popnode">

1780Crippa <em>

1781et al</em>

1782, 2008b</a>

1783), the present results of the VAMS scale showed that the SAD-PLAC group presented a significantly higher anxiety level and greater cognitive impairment, discomfort, and alert compared with the control group during the test. This was expected as the fear of speaking in public is a cardinal manifestation of SAD (<a href="#bib5" rid="bib5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659047">

1784Brunello <em>

1785et al</em>

1786, 2000</a>

1787).</p>

1788<p id="__p30">

1789Pretreatment of SAD patients with CBD significantly reduced anxiety, cognitive impairment, and discomfort in their speech performance (S) and significantly decreased alert in their anticipatory speech (A). The cognitive impairment, discomfort, and alert of SAD patients that received CBD had similar results to the HC during the SPST. These preliminary results indicate that a single dose of CBD can reduce the anxiety-enhancing effect provoked by SPST in SAD patients, indicating that this cannabinoid inhibits the fear of speaking in public, one of the main symptoms of the disorder.</p>

1790<p id="__p31">

1791The anxiolytic effects of CBD had been extensively demonstrated in animal studies and in healthy volunteers submitted to anxiety induced by several procedures, including the simulation of public speaking (<a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659061">

1792Crippa <em>

1793et al</em>

1794, 2010</a>

1795, <a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659060">

17962011</a>

1797). However, there is only one published report of the anxiolytic effect of CBD in an anxiety disorder (<a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659096">

1798Crippa <em>

1799et al</em>

1800, 2010</a>

1801, <a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659078">

18022011</a>

1803). This study was performed with SAD patients and the anxiolyic effects of CBD were detected before provoking anxiety by the tracer injection and scanning procedure of SPECT, suggesting that CBD facilitates habituation of anticipatory anxiety. The SPECT analysis of this study and of a previous one with healthy volunteers (<a href="#bib16" rid="bib16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659095">

1804Crippa <em>

1805et al</em>

1806, 2004</a>

1807) showed that the CBD effects were associated with the activity of the parahippocampal gyrus and hippocampus. Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) detected attenuated responses in the amygdala and in the cingulated cortex induced by CBD (600&#x02009;mg) during the viewing of fearful facial stimuli (<a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659086">

1808Fusar-Poli <em>

1809et al</em>

1810, 2009a</a>

1811). Moreover, CBD has shown to disrupt forward intrinsic connectivity between the amygdala and the anterior cingulate during the neural response to fearful faces (<a href="#bib25" rid="bib25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659084">

1812Fusar-Poli <em>

1813et al</em>

1814, 2009b</a>

1815). Taken together, these studies demonstrate the action of CBD in limbic and paralimbic brain areas, which are known to be associated with anxiety.</p>

1816<p id="__p32">

1817The anxiolytic action of CBD may be mediated by 5-HT<sub>

18181A</sub>

1819 receptors, as it displaces the agonist [3H]8-OHDPAT from the cloned human 5-HT1A receptor in a concentration-dependent manner and exerts an effect as an agonist at the human 5-HT1A receptor in signal-transduction studies (<a href="#bib39" rid="bib39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659079">

1820Russo <em>

1821et al</em>

1822, 2005</a>

1823). Additionally, CBD injected into the dorsolateral periaqueductal gray of rats produced anxiolytic-like effects in the elevated plus-maze and elevated T-maze, and these effects were prevented by a 5HT1A receptor antagonist (<a href="#bib41" rid="bib41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659087">

1824Soares <em>

1825et al</em>

1826, 2010</a>

1827; <a href="#bib7" rid="bib7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659055">

1828Campos and Guimaraes, 2008</a>

1829).</p>

1830<p id="__p33">

1831Another important observation of this study was that the increase of negative self-evaluation during public speaking was almost abolished by CBD. In a previous study, we suggested that the negative self-evaluation during the phobic situation of public speaking would be important for the avoidance and impairment in social functioning that support the diagnosis of SAD (Freitas-Ferrari <em>

1832et al</em>

1833, submitted). In that way, the observed effect of CBD for improving the self-evaluation during public speaking, which is one of the pivotal aspects of SAD, will influence the therapy of SAD patients.</p>

1834<p id="__p34">

1835Although physiological measures have not shown significant differences among the groups, the self-report of somatic symptoms (BSS) increased significantly only for the SAD patients who received placebo during the test. Following the same rationale as above, it is well-known that more pronounced bodily symptoms may contribute to the clinical diagnosis of SAD, and this result suggests that CBD also protects the patients from their subjective physiological abnormalities induced by the SPST.</p>

1836<p id="__p35">

1837The findings reported herein need to be interpreted with caution, given the limitations of the study. First, it would have been desirable to measure plasma levels of CBD and to relate such measurements to changes in the VAMS scores; however, it should be pointed out that previous investigations have not been able to confirm whether there is a direct relationship between plasma levels of cannabinoids, in particular CBD, and their clinical effects (<a href="#bib2" rid="bib2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659066">

1838Agurell <em>

1839et al</em>

1840, 1986</a>

1841). Another limitation refers to the size of the sample included; however, the statistical power of the data from the VAMS and SSPS was shown to be relatively robust even with small subject numbers.</p>

1842<p id="__p36">

1843An extensive list of medications for the pharmacological treatment of SAD was made available in recent years, including selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), selective serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SSNRI), antidepressants and benzodiazepines (<a href="#bib40" rid="bib40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_334520896">

1844Schneier, 2001</a>

1845). However, both SSRIs and SSNRIs have an initial activation and a long latency period of response, and benzodiazepines are limited by their potential to produce motor impairment, sedation, and to induce dependence and withdrawal symptoms following discontinuation (<a href="#bib3" rid="bib3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659092">

1846Blanco <em>

1847et al</em>

1848, 2002</a>

1849). Conversely, CBD has important advantages in comparison with the currently available pharmacological agents for the treatment of SAD, such as an early onset of action and lack of important side effects both with acute and chronic administration to healthy subjects (<a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659059">

1850Crippa <em>

1851et al</em>

1852, 2010</a>

1853, <a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659080">

18542011</a>

1855). Moreover, it was shown that repeated treatment with CBD (but not 9-THC) does not develop tolerance or dependence (<a href="#bib27" rid="bib27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659053">

1856Hayakawa <em>

1857et al</em>

1858, 2007</a>

1859) and possibly reduces drug-seeking behaviors (<a href="#bib37" rid="bib37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659057">

1860Parker <em>

1861et al</em>

1862, 2004</a>

1863; <a href="#bib38" rid="bib38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659046">

1864Ren <em>

1865et al</em>

1866, 2009</a>

1867; <a href="#bib33" rid="bib33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219659085">

1868Morgan <em>

1869et al</em>

1870, 2010</a>

1871). Thus, because of the absence of psychoactive or cognitive effects, to its safety and tolerability profiles, and to its broad pharmacological spectrum, CBD is possibly the cannabinoid that is most likely to have initial findings in anxiety translated into clinical practice.</p>

1872<p id="__p37" class="p p-last">

1873Therefore, the effects of a single dose of CBD, observed in this study in the face of one of the main SAD's phobic stimuli, is a promising indication of a rapid onset of therapeutic effect in patients with SAD. However, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, clinical trials with larger samples and chronic use are still needed to confirm these statements. Likewise, because CBD effects are biphasic, the determination of adequate treatment ranges for each disorder remains a challenge. Further research to determine the precise mechanisms of action of CBD in the different anxiety disorders is desirable and opportune.</p>
