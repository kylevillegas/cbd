694<h2>Human Body Produces Cannabinoids like CBD?</h2>

695<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The human body does produce cannabinoids. </span><b>Endogenous Cannabinoids</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> are neurotransmitters produced within our bodies that bind to cannabinoid receptors in the brain, immune system, and elsewhere. Examples include anandamide, </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-Arachidonoylglycerol"><span style="font-weight: 400;">2-arachidonoylglycerol </span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">(2-AG), </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-Arachidonoyl_dopamine"><span style="font-weight: 400;">n-arachidonoyl dopamine</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (NADA), and </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virodhamine"><span style="font-weight: 400;">virodhamine</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (OAE). </span></p>

696<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Endocannabinoids perform differently to the more well-known neurotransmitters like serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine.</span></p>

697<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Dopamine, for example, is synthesized in advance, stored in the vesicle, and in response to stimuli, is released from the </span><a href="https://teaching.ncl.ac.uk/bms/wiki/index.php/Presynaptic_and_postsynaptic_neurons"><span style="font-weight: 400;">presynaptic</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> cell, where it crosses the synapse, lands on the postsynaptic cell, and causes activation.</span></p>

698<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Endocannabinoids, on the other hand, are key components of cellular membranes that we manufacture on demand. Since endocannabinoids are hydrophobic, they cannot travel very far in the body and so their effects are localized. </span></p>

699<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Endocannabinoids also travel in the opposite direction to other neurotransmitters. They first leave the postsynaptic cell and end at the presynaptic cell where there are high concentrations of axons. Axons are responsible for the release of traditional neurotransmitters. </span></p>

700<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This allows the postsynaptic cell to control the flow of neurotransmitters coming from the presynaptic cell. </span></p>

701<h2>CBD’s Indirect Effects on the Endocannabinoid System</h2>

702<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While THC has a strong binding affinity for both CB1 and CB2 receptors, cannabidiol (CBD) has no particular binding affinity. Instead, many of the therapeutic benefits of CBD are created through indirect actions. </span></p>

703<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">These actions include activating </span><b>TRPV1 Receptors</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. These receptors are involved in regulating pain, body temperature, and inflammation. </span></p>

704<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">CBD also works to inhibit Fatty Acid Amide Hydrolase (FAAH). This inhibition creates higher levels of endocannabinoids like anandamide. Anandamide, or the bliss molecule, plays a role in the neural generation of pleasure and motivation so it’s appropriate that its name is derived from “ananda,” a Sanskrit word meaning bliss. It also performs other important functions like regulating feeding behaviors and assisting with embryo implantation during the early stages of pregnancy. </span></p>
